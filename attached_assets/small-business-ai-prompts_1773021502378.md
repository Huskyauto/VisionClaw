## How to Use These Prompts

1. Copy the prompt into ChatGPT (GPT-4 recommended) or Claude
2. Replace everything in [BRACKETS] with your info

## 📣 MARKETING & SOCIAL MEDIA

### 1. Write a Month of Social Media Posts

```
I run [BUSINESS TYPE] called [NAME] serving [TARGET CUSTOMERS] in [LOCATION/MARKET].

Write 20 social media posts for [PLATFORM]. Mix these types:
- 5 educational (tips related to our product/service)
- 5 promotional (highlighting offers, products, or services)
- 5 engagement (questions, polls, "this or that")
- 5 behind-the-scenes (team, process, story)

Keep each under [CHARACTER LIMIT]. Use a [casual/professional/fun] tone. Include emoji sparingly. No hashtags on Twitter; 3-5 hashtags on Instagram.
```

### 2. Google Business Profile Posts

```
Write 4 Google Business Profile posts for my [BUSINESS TYPE] in [CITY]. One per week for the next month.

Each post should:
- Be 150-300 words
- Include a call-to-action (call us, visit, book online)
- Mention [CURRENT PROMOTION OR SEASONAL ANGLE]
- Use local keywords naturally (don't stuff them)

My business: [BRIEF DESCRIPTION]
Our best-selling [product/service]: [DETAILS]
```

**Why this matters:** Google Business posts are free visibility that most small businesses ignore. These prompts turn 5 minutes into local SEO gold.

### 3. Email Newsletter That People Actually Open

```
Write a monthly email newsletter for my [BUSINESS TYPE] customers.

Subject line options (give me 5 to choose from):
- Use curiosity, urgency, or personal tone
- Keep under 50 characters

Newsletter structure:
1. Personal greeting + one sentence about what's new
2. One valuable tip related to [INDUSTRY]
3. Featured [product/service/offer] with clear CTA
4. Quick FAQ or myth-busting
5. Sign-off with personality

Tone: [DESCRIBE]. Length: under 400 words. We want readers to feel like they're hearing from a trusted friend, not a corporation.
```

### 4. Respond to a Negative Review

```
A customer left this review for my [BUSINESS TYPE]:

"[PASTE THE REVIEW]"

Write a professional, empathetic response that:
1. Acknowledges their frustration without being defensive
2. Apologizes for the specific issue (not a generic "sorry")
3. Briefly explains what happened or what we're doing to fix it
4. Offers to make it right (specific action, not just "contact us")
5. Keeps it under 100 words
6. Ends on a warm, inviting note

Do NOT sound corporate or scripted. Sound like a real business owner who cares.
```

### 5. Local SEO Blog Post

```
Write a 600-word blog post for my [BUSINESS TYPE] website targeting the keyword "[KEYWORD — e.g., best plumber in Austin]".

Requirements:
- Use the keyword naturally 3-4 times (title, first paragraph, one subheading, conclusion)
- Include references to [CITY/NEIGHBORHOOD] landmarks or context
- Add practical value — don't just sell, teach something
- End with a clear CTA to [call/book/visit]
- Suggest a meta description (under 155 characters)
- Conversational tone — we're local experts, not Wikipedia

Our specialties: [LIST 2-3 THINGS YOU'RE KNOWN FOR]
```

## 💰 SALES & CUSTOMER COMMUNICATION

### 6. Follow-Up Email After a Quote

```
A potential customer asked for a quote for [SERVICE/PRODUCT]. I sent it [X DAYS] ago and haven't heard back.

Write a follow-up email that:
- Is under 75 words
- Doesn't sound pushy or desperate
- Adds one new piece of value (tip, testimonial, or limited offer)
- Has a clear, easy-to-answer question as the CTA
- Subject line that gets opened (not "Following up")

The quote was for: [BRIEF DESCRIPTION]
Price: $[AMOUNT]
```

### 7. Upsell Existing Customers

```
I want to email my existing customers about [NEW PRODUCT/SERVICE/ADD-ON].

They already bought [WHAT THEY PREVIOUSLY PURCHASED] from us.

Write a short email (under 150 words) that:
- Thanks them for being a customer
- Introduces the new offering as a natural next step
- Explains one specific benefit in their language
- Includes a special "existing customer" incentive
- Feels like a recommendation, not a sales pitch
```

### 8. Create a Referral Request

```
Write a message I can send to happy customers asking for referrals.

Context: I run [BUSINESS TYPE]. The customer recently [completed purchase/finished project/had a positive experience].

Make it:
- Personal and genuine (not templated-feeling)
- Under 100 words
- Mention a specific referral incentive: [INCENTIVE — e.g., $25 credit, 10% off next service]
- Easy to act on (give them exact language they can forward)
- Available in 2 versions: email and text message
```

### 9. Handle a Price Objection

```
A potential customer said: "[THEIR OBJECTION — e.g., 'That's more than I expected' or 'Your competitor charges less']"

My [product/service]: [DESCRIPTION]
My price: $[AMOUNT]
Competitor price they mentioned: $[AMOUNT] (if known)

Write 3 response options:
1. Value-focused (explain what they get for the price)
2. Flexibility-focused (offer a modified scope or payment plan)
3. Social proof-focused (reference results from similar customers)

Keep each under 100 words. Conversational, not salesy.
```

### 10. Write a Proposal/Pitch

```
I need to write a proposal for a potential [CLIENT TYPE] who needs [SERVICE].

Details:
- Their problem: [WHAT THEY TOLD YOU]
- My proposed solution: [YOUR APPROACH]
- Timeline: [ESTIMATED DURATION]
- Price: $[AMOUNT]

Write a one-page proposal with these sections:
1. Understanding Your Needs (show I listened)
2. My Approach (what I'll do, in plain language)
3. What You Get (specific deliverables, bulleted)
4. Timeline & Investment
5. Why Me (2-3 sentences of differentiation)
6. Next Steps (clear CTA)

Tone: confident but not arrogant. Under 500 words.
```

## 📋 OPERATIONS & ADMIN

### 11. Write Job Posting That Attracts Good People

```
I need to hire a [POSITION] for my [BUSINESS TYPE] in [LOCATION/REMOTE].

Write a job posting that stands out from generic ones:
- Open with what makes this role exciting (not company history)
- List 5-7 responsibilities (start each with an action verb)
- List requirements: split into "must have" (3-4) and "nice to have" (2-3)
- Include pay range: $[RANGE]
- Mention 3 real benefits/perks: [LIST THEM]
- End with how to apply and what to include

Tone: [casual/professional]. We're a [SIZE] team that values [CULTURE TRAITS].
Don't use: "rockstar," "ninja," "fast-paced environment," "wear many hats."
```

### 12. Create Standard Operating Procedure (SOP)

```
Write an SOP for [SPECIFIC PROCESS — e.g., "handling a customer return," "onboarding a new client," "closing the shop at night"].

My business: [TYPE AND BRIEF CONTEXT]

Format:
- Title and purpose (one sentence on why this exists)
- Numbered step-by-step instructions
- Include decision points ("If X, then do Y. If not, do Z.")
- Note common mistakes to avoid
- Time estimate for the whole process
- Who is responsible for each major step

Write it so a new employee with zero context could follow it perfectly on day one.
```

### 13. Employee Performance Review Talking Points

```
I need to give a performance review to [EMPLOYEE ROLE].

Positives: [LIST 2-3 THINGS THEY DO WELL]
Areas for improvement: [LIST 1-2 THINGS]
Goal for next quarter: [WHAT YOU WANT THEM TO FOCUS ON]

Write talking points that:
- Start with genuine, specific praise (not generic "great job")
- Frame improvements as growth opportunities, not criticisms
- Include one specific, measurable goal for next 90 days
- End with asking for their input ("What support do you need from me?")

Keep it conversational — these are talking points, not a script.
```

### 14. Vendor Negotiation Email

```
I want to negotiate [better pricing/terms/delivery schedule] with my [VENDOR TYPE] for [PRODUCT/SERVICE THEY PROVIDE].

Current terms: [WHAT YOU'RE PAYING NOW]
What I want: [YOUR IDEAL TERMS]
Leverage I have: [e.g., "I've been a customer for 3 years," "I can commit to larger volumes," "I have a competing quote"]

Write a professional email that:
- Leads with the relationship value
- Makes a specific, reasonable ask
- Offers something in return (commitment, volume, prompt payment)
- Leaves room for negotiation
- Is under 150 words
```

### 15. Quarterly Business Review (for Yourself)

```
Help me do a quarterly review of my [BUSINESS TYPE].

Last quarter's numbers:
- Revenue: $[X]
- Expenses: $[X]
- New customers: [X]
- [OTHER KEY METRIC]: [X]

What went well: [LIST 2-3 WINS]
What didn't work: [LIST 1-2 PROBLEMS]
Biggest challenge right now: [DESCRIBE]

Based on this, give me:
1. Three things to KEEP doing
2. Two things to STOP or change
3. One thing to START experimenting with
4. A simple 90-day goal I can track weekly
5. The ONE number I should obsess over this quarter

Be direct. I want honest analysis, not cheerleading.
```

## 💡 STRATEGY & PLANNING

### 16. Analyze My Competition

```
I run a [BUSINESS TYPE] in [LOCATION/MARKET]. My top 3 competitors are:

1. [NAME + WEBSITE]
2. [NAME + WEBSITE]
3. [NAME + WEBSITE]

Based on what you can find, analyze:
- What they do well (that I should learn from)
- Gaps in their offering (opportunities for me)
- Their pricing strategy vs. mine ($[MY PRICE])
- Their online presence strengths and weaknesses
- One specific thing I could do to differentiate

Give me actionable takeaways, not generic advice.
```

### 17. Create a 90-Day Growth Plan

```
My [BUSINESS TYPE] currently makes $[X]/month. I want to reach $[TARGET]/month in 90 days.

Current situation:
- Main revenue source: [WHAT]
- Number of customers/clients: [X]
- Average transaction value: $[X]
- Marketing I'm currently doing: [LIST]
- Biggest bottleneck: [WHAT'S HOLDING YOU BACK]

Create a 90-day plan broken into 3 phases (one per month):
- Month 1: Quick wins (what can move the needle this month?)
- Month 2: Build systems (what processes need to exist?)
- Month 3: Scale what works (double down on winners)

For each month, give me 3 specific actions with expected impact. No fluff.
```

### 18. Price My Product/Service

```
Help me figure out the right price for [PRODUCT/SERVICE].

Details:
- What it is: [DESCRIPTION]
- What it costs me to deliver: $[COST]
- Current price: $[X] (or "I haven't priced it yet")
- Competitor prices: $[RANGE] (if known)
- My target customer: [WHO THEY ARE]
- Perceived value/result for customer: [WHAT THEY GET]

Give me:
1. A recommended price with reasoning
2. A pricing tier structure (basic/standard/premium) if applicable
3. Whether I should price by project, hourly, or monthly retainer
4. One psychological pricing tactic I should use
5. How to present the price so it feels like a no-brainer
```

### 19. Write My Business's Elevator Pitch

```
I need a clear, compelling elevator pitch for my [BUSINESS TYPE].

Who I serve: [TARGET CUSTOMER]
What I do: [SERVICE/PRODUCT]
What makes me different: [1-2 KEY DIFFERENTIATORS]
The result I deliver: [SPECIFIC OUTCOME FOR CUSTOMERS]

Write 3 versions:
1. The 10-second version (for "what do you do?" at a party)
2. The 30-second version (for networking events)
3. The website hero section version (headline + subheadline + CTA button text)

Use plain language. No jargon. No buzzwords. A 12-year-old should understand what I do.
```

### 20. Plan a Product/Service Launch

```
I'm launching [NEW PRODUCT/SERVICE] for my [BUSINESS TYPE] in [TIMEFRAME].

Details:
- What it is: [DESCRIPTION]
- Price: $[AMOUNT]
- Target buyer: [WHO]
- Why it's different: [DIFFERENTIATOR]
- My existing audience: [SIZE AND WHERE — email list, social, foot traffic]

Create a launch plan with:
1. Pre-launch (2 weeks before): How to build anticipation
2. Launch day: Exact steps and messages to send
3. Launch week: Follow-up sequence
4. Post-launch: How to sustain momentum

Include specific email subject lines, social post ideas, and one creative "launch event" idea that fits a small business budget ($0-100).
```

## 🎁 BONUS: The "Save Me 30 Minutes" Prompts


### 22. Write Thank-You Note Variations
```
Write 5 different thank-you messages I can send to customers after [purchase/service]. Each should feel personal and unique — not copy-paste. Include one that asks for a Google review naturally. Under 50 words each.
```

### 23. Create a Seasonal Promotion
```
It's [SEASON/HOLIDAY]. Write a promotional offer for my [BUSINESS TYPE] that feels festive without being cheesy. Include: the offer, email subject line, one social post, and a 3-sentence script for telling walk-in customers.
```

### 24. Summarize a Long Document
```
Summarize this [contract/lease/policy/report] into plain English bullet points. Flag anything unusual, risky, or that I should ask a lawyer about. I'm a small business owner, not a legal expert. [PASTE DOCUMENT]
```

### 25. End-of-Day Brain Dump Organizer
```
Here's everything on my mind about my business right now: [BRAIN DUMP — just type stream of consciousness]. Organize this into: (1) urgent tasks for tomorrow, (2) important but not urgent, (3) ideas to explore later, (4) things to delegate or drop. Be ruthless about what doesn't matter.
```

## 💪 Getting the Most Out of These Prompts

1. **Be specific.** "I run a bakery in Portland" beats "I have a food business." The more context you give, the better the output.

2. **Iterate.** First output is a draft. Say "make it more casual" or "shorter" or "add more urgency." AI is a conversation, not a vending machine.

3. **Build a prompt library.** Save your best customized versions. You'll reuse them monthly.

4. **Combine prompts.** Use #17 (growth plan) to set strategy, then #1 (social posts) and #3 (newsletter) to execute it. They work as a system.

5. **Update quarterly.** Swap in new products, seasonal angles, and fresh customer language every 90 days.
